# Flood Retention Parks, Water Plazas and Detention Basins

## Description

Flood parks are multi-functional public spaces designed to temporarily store stormwater during heavy rainfall events while functioning as recreational areas during dry weather. They represent a blue-green infrastructure approach that integrates flood risk management with urban amenity, replacing or supplementing conventional grey stormwater infrastructure (pipes, culverts, underground tanks) with visible, accessible landscape features.

Key modalities include:
- **Water plazas**: Sunken public squares that fill with stormwater during rain events and drain within hours, returning to recreational use (Rotterdam model)
- **Dry detention parks**: Parks with depressed areas or sports fields designed to flood temporarily, draining within 24--72 hours
- **Wet detention parks**: Parks incorporating permanent water features (ponds, lakes) that expand during storm events, providing both amenity and storage
- **Flood retention parks**: Larger-scale parks along rivers or floodplains designed to absorb peak flows and release water slowly
- **Constructed wetland parks**: Engineered wetlands that combine flood storage with water quality treatment and habitat creation
- **Resilient waterfront parks**: Coastal or riverside parks intentionally designed to be inundated with minimal damage during storm surge or river flooding

## How It Works

Flood parks operate on the principle of controlled temporary inundation. During dry conditions, the spaces function as conventional parks with sports fields, playgrounds, seating areas, and paths. When rainfall exceeds the capacity of the conventional drainage system, stormwater is directed into the park via gravity or active diversion infrastructure (weirs, overflow channels). The park's topography -- designed with sunken areas, berms, and graded surfaces -- contains and slows the water. Vegetation, permeable surfaces, and engineered soil media filter pollutants during infiltration. After the rain event, water drains via gravity outlets (typically throttled to reduce downstream peak flows), infiltrates into groundwater, or is pumped out. Materials, furniture, and plantings within the flood zone are selected for submersion tolerance.

### Benefit-Cost Ratio

- Blue-green infrastructure network (NYC study, Rebuild by Design/Ramboll): **2:1 return on investment** for a 10-year storm design in 2050, accounting for avoided flood damages, cleanup costs, and resilience value
- Flood parks vs. underground storage: Surface detention costs 60--90% less per m3 of storage while providing recreational co-benefits
- Property value: Parks with water features increase adjacent property values 5--20%, generating additional municipal tax revenue

## Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

### Stormwater Retention
- **Volume per unit area**: 500--3,000 m3/ha depending on design depth (0.3--1.5m average flood depth)
- **Benthemplein water plaza (Rotterdam)**: 1,800 m3 (1.7 million liters) on a 9,500 m2 site
- **Peak flow reduction**: 50--90% of peak discharge compared to conventional drainage
- **Drain time**: 6--72 hours depending on design (water plazas <12h; large detention parks 24--72h)
- **Design storm**: Typically sized for 10-year to 100-year return period events

### Water Quality
- **Total suspended solids removal**: 60--90% through settling and filtration
- **Nutrient removal (N, P)**: 30--65% in wet detention; 50--80% in constructed wetland parks
- **Heavy metals removal**: 40--70% through sedimentation and plant uptake
- **Bacterial load reduction**: 50--90% with sufficient residence time and UV exposure

### Flood Risk Reduction
- **Downstream flood depth reduction**: 10--30 cm for neighborhood-scale parks in a 25-year event
- **Damage cost avoidance**: $50,000--$500,000 per event depending on urban context and park size
- **Insurance premium reduction**: 5--15% for properties within managed floodplain park networks

### Cooling Effect
- **Surface temperature reduction**: 2--5C in park area vs. surrounding hardscape
- **Evaporative cooling from water features**: Additional 1--3C during wet periods
- **Effective cooling radius**: 50--200m beyond park boundary depending on size and wind

### Recreational and Social
- **Park usage**: Well-designed flood parks attract 80--95% of the usage of conventional parks during dry weather
- **Community satisfaction**: 70--85% approval ratings in post-implementation surveys (Rotterdam, Copenhagen examples)
- **Active recreation capacity**: Dry detention areas can accommodate sports fields, skate parks, playgrounds during 90--95% of the year

### Carbon
- **Sequestration**: 2--8 t CO2eq/ha/yr depending on vegetation density (lower than forests due to open character)
- **Avoided emissions**: Reduced pumping energy vs. deep tunnel/underground systems; 30--60% lower embodied carbon than grey alternatives

## Climate Benefits

### Adaptation
- Direct flood damage reduction through controlled temporary storage of peak flows
- Groundwater recharge improving base flows during drought
- Urban heat island mitigation through evapotranspiration and water surfaces
- Water quality improvement reducing pollutant loads to receiving waters
- Community resilience through visible, understood flood infrastructure

### Mitigation
- Lower embodied carbon than equivalent grey infrastructure (concrete tunnels, underground tanks)
- Reduced energy for pumping compared to deep drainage systems
- Carbon sequestration in park vegetation and soils
- Reduced vehicle trips when parks serve as neighborhood amenities accessible by walking

## Optimal Site Conditions

- **Topography**: Natural low points, floodplains, or areas where gravity drainage from surrounding catchment is feasible; avoid sites requiring extensive pumping
- **Soil**: Permeable soils (sandy loam, loam) preferred for infiltration-based designs; clay soils require lined detention or constructed wetland approach
- **Groundwater**: Water table should be >1m below basin floor for dry detention; wet ponds tolerate high water table
- **Catchment size**: Typically serves 5--50 ha of contributing catchment area per detention park
- **Flood risk zone**: Ideal in areas with documented pluvial or fluvial flood risk where conventional drainage is insufficient
- **Urban context**: Proximity to residential areas for recreational co-benefits; minimum 0.5 ha for meaningful storage and park function
- **Contamination**: Avoid sites with contaminated soils unless lined; stormwater from industrial catchments may need pre-treatment
- **Utilities**: Subsurface utility mapping essential; conflicts with sewers, pipes, and cables common in urban retrofits

## Risks and Failure Modes

1. **Undersizing**: Storage volume insufficient for design storm leads to overtopping and downstream flooding; climate change may increase design storm intensity
2. **Sediment accumulation**: Without regular maintenance (every 5--15 years), basins lose storage capacity; cost of dredging can be significant
3. **Mosquito breeding**: Standing water in wet detention areas creates public health risk if not properly managed with circulation, fish, or larvicide
4. **Public safety during floods**: Rapid inundation of recreational areas poses drowning risk; requires warning systems, barriers, and public education
5. **Contaminated stormwater**: First flush from roads carries heavy metals, hydrocarbons, and microplastics; park soils may accumulate pollutants over time
6. **Clogged outlets**: Debris blocking throttle structures or overflow pipes causes uncontrolled flooding; requires regular inspection
7. **Community resistance**: "My park floods?" -- perception of degraded amenity if community engagement is insufficient during design
8. **Structural failure**: Embankments, outlet structures, or berms can fail during extreme events exceeding design capacity
9. **Maintenance neglect**: Flood infrastructure in parks often falls between municipal departments (parks vs. drainage), leading to responsibility gaps
10. **Land competition**: In dense cities, dedicating scarce land to occasional flood storage faces political pressure from development interests

## Brazilian and Latin American Examples

### Porto Alegre, Brazil -- Post-2024 Flood NBS Strategy
- After the catastrophic May 2024 flood (500mm rainfall, 1.5 million affected), the city is developing a green-grey hybrid flood strategy
- Existing grey system: 65 km of dykes, 14 floodgates, 22 pump stations -- all of which proved insufficient
- Proposed NBS elements include flood retention parks along the Guaiba River tributaries, rain gardens, and permeable surface retrofits
- Dutch water engineers (Deltares) supporting redesign with blue-green infrastructure integrated into park systems
- Directly relevant to NBS Project Preparation platform as Porto Alegre is the sample city
- Source: Dutch Water Sector (2024), CDRI (2025)

### Curitiba, Brazil -- Flood Control Parks
- Pioneer of the "flood park" concept since the 1970s under Jaime Lerner's urban planning
- **Parque Barigui** (1.4 million m2): Linear park along the Barigui River serving as flood detention while providing recreation for 1+ million annual visitors
- **Parque Tingui**: Combined flood retention, cultural heritage (Ukrainian memorial), and ecological corridor along the Barigui River
- Parks designed to absorb river flood peaks, with surrounding areas graded to allow controlled inundation of low-value zones (sports fields, meadows)
- Cost-effective approach: land acquisition along floodplains was cheaper than building underground drainage
- Source: Curitiba Urban Planning Institute (IPPUC)

### Buenos Aires, Argentina -- Parque de la Estacion and Riachuelo Basin
- Urban parks incorporating stormwater detention as part of the Matanza-Riachuelo basin cleanup
- New parks in southern Buenos Aires designed with sunken areas for flood storage during pampero storms
- Integrates with sewer infrastructure upgrades to reduce combined sewer overflows
- Source: ACUMAR (Matanza-Riachuelo Basin Authority)

### Recife, Brazil -- Capibaribe Park Project
- 30 km linear park system along the Capibaribe River combining flood management with urban regeneration
- Designed to accommodate regular flood events while providing public space in one of Brazil's most flood-prone cities
- Includes mangrove restoration zones that serve as natural flood buffers
- Source: Grupo de Pesquisa Inciti (UFPE)

### Bogota, Colombia -- Parque Tercer Milenio and Wetland Parks
- Wetland park system (humedales) integrated into the city's stormwater management
- 15 recognized urban wetlands serve dual function as flood storage and biodiversity reserves
- Humedal Juan Amarillo (220 ha): Largest urban wetland in the Andes, providing flood storage for the Juan Amarillo River
- Source: Secretaria Distrital de Ambiente

### Copenhagen, Denmark -- Enghaveparken (Reference Project)
- While not Latin American, widely cited as a model for tropical adaptation
- 1.4 ha historic park retrofitted to store 22,600 m3 of stormwater during cloudburst events
- Cost: EUR 13 million ($14.5M); sports fields and lawns designed to flood to 1m depth
- Fully operational within hours of draining; used as normal park 95% of the year
- Source: City of Copenhagen Climate Adaptation Plan
