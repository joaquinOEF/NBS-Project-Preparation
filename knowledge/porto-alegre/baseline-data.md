- NBS impact on emissions is primarily through AFOLU sector (carbon sequestration) and indirect energy/transport effects

## Land Cover and Green Space

### Urban Forest and Tree Canopy
- Approximately **1 million trees** in the municipality (IBGE Census data)
- **76.5%** of households located on streets with at least one tree (above national average of 66%)
- Porto Alegre ranks among the **15 most forested cities in Brazil**
- Highest pollutant sequestration among 25 Brazilian cities studied: 15.67 tonnes CO annually
- Afforestation Master Plan (2006) targets 40% plant coverage on public sidewalks

### Green Areas
- Municipality area: 496.68 km2
- Biome transition zone: Atlantic Forest (hills and ridges) and Pampa (lowlands)
- The city concentrates **28% of the native flora of Rio Grande do Sul** with approximately 9,288 species
- Key green areas include parks, conservation units, APPs, and private green spaces

### Land Cover Changes
- Rapid urbanization has significantly reduced permeable surfaces
- Loss of wetlands (banhados) and floodplains to development
- Impermeabilization of urban surfaces reduces natural drainage capacity
- Post-2024 flood satellite analysis identified areas of vegetation disturbance

## Drainage Infrastructure

### Flood Protection System
- **System origin**: Installed early 1970s by DNOS (Departamento Nacional de Obras e Saneamento)
- **68 km** of external and internal dikes and barriers along Guaiba and Gravatai banks
- **Muro da Maua**: 2.6 km long, 3 m high concrete retaining wall protecting downtown Centro Historico between coach terminal and Praia das Belas
- **14 iron floodgates**: Along external dikes, avenues, highways, and the Muro da Maua; closed when Guaiba level rises to prevent flooding in protected urban area
- **23 pump stations (casas de bomba)**: 83 pumps with total capacity of 159,000 L/s, designed to move interior floodwater to Guaiba Lake and Gravatai River
- **~2,500 km of storm sewers** and **~120,000 collection points** (storm drains and inspection wells)
- **Design capacity**: Water levels up to 6.0 m (based on 1941 maximum of 4.76 m + 1.25 m safety margin)
- **Alert/flood levels**: Guaiba alert at 2.5 m; flood level at 3.0 m

### System Condition (pre-2024 flood)
- Deferred maintenance since DEP dissolution in 2017
- Pump stations operating at reduced capacity
- Floodgates with mechanical failures
- Estimated **R$ 4 billion** needed for full system rehabilitation
- **R$ 400 million** for pump station repairs alone
- Annual O&M requirement: **R$ 200 million/year** post-rehabilitation

### Combined Sewer System
- Porto Alegre operates a largely combined sewer system in older urban areas
- Stormwater and sewage share the same pipes in many neighborhoods
- During heavy rainfall, combined sewer overflows (CSOs) discharge untreated sewage into waterways
- Separation of systems is ongoing but far from complete

### Key Drainage Challenges
- Arroio Diluvio: Major urban stream, heavily channelized, receives significant pollution
- Low-lying neighborhoods (Humaita, Navegantes, Ilhas) below Guaiba flood levels
- Pump dependency for drainage in large portions of the urbanized area
- Climate change increasing rainfall intensity beyond original design parameters

## Water Quality

### Lago Guaiba
- Major water body (496 km2 total surface area) serving as Porto Alegre's primary water supply source
- Receives waters from five rivers (Jacui, Sinos, Gravatai, Cai, Taquari) before flowing to Lagoa dos Patos
- **Pollution sources**:
  - Deficient sewage treatment (combined sewer overflows)
  - Industrial discharges from metropolitan region
  - Agricultural runoff from upstream basins
  - Urban stormwater carrying pollutants
- **Pollutant classes**: Organic matter, heavy metals, pesticides, emerging contaminants
- Despite pollution, Guaiba remains the primary water supply source and supports fisheries
- FEPAM and DMAE monitor water quality parameters

### Arroio Diluvio
- Primary urban waterway running through the city center along Avenida Ipiranga
- Heavily polluted by untreated sewage, solid waste, and urban runoff
- Subject of the "Regenera Diluvio" restoration project
- NBS opportunity: riparian buffer restoration, biofiltration, constructed wetlands

### Air Quality
- Network of 5 air monitoring stations (partnership: SMAMUS, FEPAM, UFRGS, Petrobras)
- Porto Alegre's urban forest contributes significantly to air pollutant sequestration
- Construction activities are a notable pollution source (CO2, particulate matter)

## Biodiversity

### Atlantic Forest Remnants
- Forest patches concentrated on Porto Alegre's 40 hills (morros) and ridges
- Morro Santana (311 m elevation) hosts significant forest remnants and is a UFRGS ecological research site
- Remaining forest fragments function as ecological stepping stones but face fragmentation pressure
- The city's position at the Atlantic Forest-Pampa transition creates exceptional urban biodiversity

### Banhados (Wetlands)
- Historically extensive wetlands along the Guaiba and tributary floodplains
- Many have been filled for urban development, reducing natural flood buffering
- Remaining banhados are critical for biodiversity, water filtration, and flood attenuation
- Reserva Biologica do Lami preserves banhado ecosystems in the south of the municipality

### Delta do Jacui
- **Parque Estadual Delta do Jacui (PEDJ)**: Full-protection conservation unit created 1976, area expanded 1979
- **Park area**: 14,242.05 hectares (adjusted in 2005 when APA was created)
- **APA Estadual Delta do Jacui (APAEDJ)**: Sustainable use conservation unit created 2005, encompassing 22,826.39 hectares (includes the PEDJ within its limits)
- Spans Porto Alegre, Canoas, Nova Santa Rita, Triunfo, Charqueadas, and Eldorado do Sul
- Located at confluence of Jacui, Taquari, Cai, Sinos, and Gravatai rivers
- Encompasses two biomes: Atlantic Forest and Pampa
- Delta waters function as a natural filter for Guaiba, contributing to potability
- Supports fisheries and maintains good fish productivity
- Critical biodiversity corridor connecting upstream ecosystems with the Guaiba
- **Banhado do Gravatai**: Major wetland system along Gravatai River north of Porto Alegre; critical for flood buffering and water quality but increasingly threatened by urban expansion from Canoas and Cachoeirinha

### Key Species and Habitats
- Native flora: approximately 9,288 species documented in the municipal territory
- Riparian forests along streams and the Guaiba shoreline
- Grassland ecosystems (Pampa biome) in southern areas
- Aquatic ecosystems in the Guaiba, delta, and urban lakes

### Hazard Risk Scoring (gridService.ts)
The grid computes three risk scores per 1km cell:

| Hazard | Formula | Key Drivers |
|---|---|---|
| **Flood** | 0.45×floodplain + 0.20×elevation + 0.20×imperviousness + 0.15×river_proximity | Low-lying + impervious + near water |

| **Landslide** | 0.55×slope + 0.30×urban_creep + 0.15×(1−canopy) | Steep + expanding + deforested |

## Data Gaps and Needs

1. **Updated GHG inventory**: The 2nd inventory covers only through 2019; post-2024 data needed to assess flood impact on emissions
2. **Flood inundation maps**: No frequency/depth data from JRC Global Flood Hazard or local flood studies
3. **Climate projections**: Localized scenarios for 2030/2050 (rainfall intensity, temperature, sea level) — could integrate ISIMIP or WorldBank data
4. **Soil permeability mapping**: Critical for green infrastructure siting (bioswales, rain gardens, infiltration areas)
5. **Water quality time series**: Comprehensive Guaíba and tributary monitoring data for NBS impact assessment
6. **Biodiversity baseline**: Species inventories in target NBS areas needed for restoration planning
7. **Slope derivatives**: Landslide score currently uses placeholder (S=0) — needs actual slope computation from Copernicus DEM
