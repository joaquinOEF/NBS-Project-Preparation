# Porto Alegre - Climate Risks

## The May 2024 Catastrophic Floods

### Event Overview
The May 2024 floods in Rio Grande do Sul were the **worst natural disaster in the state's recorded history** and among the worst in Brazil in over 80 years. Porto Alegre was at the epicenter.

### Key Statistics

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Deaths (statewide) | 183 |
| People displaced | 422,473 |
| Total people affected | ~2.4 million |
| Municipalities affected | 478 of 497 (96%) |
| Municipalities with flood damage | 431 |
| Economic damages (statewide) | >USD 15 billion |
| Business losses | 90% of businesses suffered partial or total losses |
| Peak water level (Porto Alegre) | 535 cm (5.35 m) |
| Previous record (1941) | 476 cm (4.76 m) |
| Territory of Porto Alegre flooded | ~48% |

### Chronology
- **Late April 2024**: Intense rainfall begins across RS, driven by weakening El Nino steering cold fronts toward the region
- **April 30**: >150 mm of rainfall in parts of RS in 24 hours (INMET)
- **May 1-2**: Porto Alegre experiences historic flooding; Guaiba level surpasses 1941 record by 59 cm
- **May 2024 total precipitation in Porto Alegre**: 513.6 mm -- almost 5x the monthly average
- **First 12 days of May**: Up to 333.1 mm, nearly 3x the 1991-2020 monthly average
- **Infrastructure collapse**: 19 of 23 pumping stations shut down; Salgado Filho Airport closed; Metro suspended; buses halted
- **14 de Julho dam**: Partial collapse of hydroelectric dam on Das Antas River (between Cotipora and Bento Goncalves) killed at least 30 people

### Contributing Factors
1. **Climate change**: With 1.2C global warming, the event became approximately **2x as likely** or equivalently **12% more intense** (World Weather Attribution)
2. **El Nino**: Weakening El Nino phase steered cold fronts and increased atmospheric instability over southern Brazil
3. **Urbanization and impermeabilization**: Decades of urban sprawl reduced natural water absorption capacity
4. **Deforestation in the Guaiba basin**: Reduced upstream retention capacity
5. **Infrastructure failure**: Porto Alegre's flood protection system failed catastrophically -- gaps of up to 10 cm between floodgates and walls; stolen floodgate motors never replaced; pumps that did not function
6. **Institutional degradation**: DEP (Department of Storm Sewers) dissolved in 2017; over 10 years of reduced investment and human resources at DMAE

### Areas Most Affected in Porto Alegre
- **Centro Historico**: Downtown flooded despite Muro da Maua protection
- **Arquipelago (Ilhas)**: Island neighborhoods completely submerged
- **Humaita-Navegantes**: Low-lying northern areas, severe flooding
- **Sarandi**: Major flooding in northern zone
- **Irregular settlements**: 240 favelas, 40 quilombola communities, 5 Indigenous villages directly impacted statewide
- **Salgado Filho Airport**: Remained closed for months after the flood

### Social Impact
- 47% of families earning up to two minimum wages lost their home, furniture, appliances, or means of subsistence
- The number of displaced people statewide reached 877,729
- Low-income and marginalized communities disproportionately affected

## Historical Flood Events

| Year | Peak Level (Guaiba) | Notes |
|------|---------------------|-------|
| 1873 | ~4.3 m (est.) | Third-largest historical flood |
| 1941 | 4.76 m | Previous record; led to construction of flood protection system |
| Sept 2023 | Significant | Fourth-largest recorded flood |
| Nov 2023 | Significant | Fifth-largest recorded flood |
| May 2024 | 5.35 m | All-time record; catastrophic |

**Critical pattern**: Of the five biggest floods ever recorded in Porto Alegre, **three occurred in the nine months from September 2023 to May 2024**, indicating a clear acceleration of extreme events.

## Heat Waves and Urban Heat Island

### Current Conditions
- Porto Alegre exhibits significant **urban heat island (UHI)** effect, with documented intensification over the past two decades
- The city is among Brazilian cities showing higher intensities of diurnal UHI
- Recent school closures in Porto Alegre due to persistent extreme heat events
- UHI is exacerbated by high impermeabilization, concrete/asphalt dominance, and insufficient green cover

### Heat Risk Factors
- Subtropical climate with hot, humid summers (temperatures regularly exceeding 35C)
- Dense urban core with limited tree canopy
- Vulnerable populations (elderly, low-income) concentrated in heat-exposed areas
- Heat-related vector-borne disease risk (dengue, other arboviroses)

## Landslide-Prone Areas

- Porto Alegre's topography includes **40 morros (hills)** with steep slopes
- Irregular settlements (favelas) built on hillsides face combined landslide and flood risk
- The 2024 floods triggered landslides and mudslides across the state
- Key risk areas: Morro da Cruz, Morro Santa Teresa, Morro da Policia, and hillside neighborhoods in Lomba do Pinheiro, Partenon, and Gloria regions
- Highly densely populated regions in Southern and Southeastern Brazil are among the most exposed to landslides and floods, with impacts projected to worsen with further warming

## Climate Projections

### Precipitation
- Southern Brazil is projected to see **significant increases in annual precipitation totals** and **annual 5-day precipitation maximums** by 2050 (IPCC AR6)
- Extreme rainfall events are becoming more frequent and intense; the clustering of events (3 of 5 worst floods in 9 months) suggests a shift in extremes regime
- El Nino/La Nina cycles continue to amplify variability

### Temperature
- Global projections indicate continued warming for the region
- Urban heat island intensification expected as urbanization continues
- Heat wave frequency and duration projected to increase significantly -- some projections suggest Brazilian cities could face extreme heat events up to 15 times per year under high-emissions scenarios

### Six Main Climate Threats (per PLAC Risk Analysis)
1. **Floods** (inundacoes/enchentes)
2. **Storms** (tempestades)
3. **Landslides** (deslizamentos)
4. **Heat waves** (ondas de calor)
5. **Droughts** (secas)
6. **Vector-borne disease proliferation** (arboviroses)

## Flood Protection System

### Infrastructure Components
- **Muro da Maua**: 3 m high, 2.647 km long wall protecting downtown
- **14 floodgates**: Control water entry points through the wall
- **68 km of dikes**: Ring dike system around low-lying areas
- **19 pumping stations (casas de bomba)**: Drain interior water back to Guaiba
- **~2,500 km of storm sewers**: Municipal drainage network
- **~120,000 collection points**: Storm drains and inspection wells

### Design Parameters
- System designed for water levels up to **6.0 m** (based on 1941 maximum of 4.76 m + 1.25 m safety margin)
- **Guaiba alert level**: 2.5 m
- **Guaiba flood level**: 3.0 m
- The 2024 event at 5.35 m was within the design envelope but the system failed due to maintenance neglect and equipment failures

### System Failures in 2024
- 19 of 23 pumping stations shut down (flooding and electrical risks)
- Gaps of up to 10 cm between floodgates and the wall
- Stolen floodgate motors never replaced
- Pumps that did not function
- The system was "not designed for extreme events" per some experts, but the core failure was maintenance and institutional neglect

## Implications for NBS

The convergence of climate risks in Porto Alegre creates a strong case for NBS interventions:
- **Upstream watershed restoration**: Reduce peak flows into the Guaiba
- **Urban green infrastructure**: Increase permeable surfaces, reduce runoff
- **Riparian buffer restoration**: Along Arroio Diluvio and other urban streams
- **Wetland and floodplain restoration**: Natural flood retention on Guaiba islands
- **Urban tree canopy expansion**: Mitigate heat island, reduce stormwater runoff
- **Hillside stabilization**: Bioengineering approaches for landslide-prone morros
- **Mangrove/riparian vegetation**: Along Guaiba shoreline for erosion control
