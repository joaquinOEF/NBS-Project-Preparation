# NBS Impact Benchmarks

## Restoration Costs by Ecosystem Type

### Atlantic Forest (Brazil)
- **Average restoration cost**: US$ 5,000-6,000 per hectare (2023 data)
- **Range**: US$ 2,000-10,000/ha depending on micro-biome, density, species diversity, and degradation level
- **Key cost drivers**: Seedling production, site preparation, labor, maintenance (3-5 years), monitoring
- Costs and benefits of restoration remain poorly quantified according to systematic literature reviews (Schimetka, 2024)
- Seedling costs constitute 20-40% of total restoration investment

### Brazilian Restoration Cost Benchmarks (IDB/WWF data)
- **Passive restoration**: BRL 22,700/ha (~US$ 4,200/ha at 2021 prices)
- **Active restoration**: BRL 34,980/ha (~US$ 6,500/ha at 2021 prices)
- **Latin America 20M ha restoration projection**: USD 1,140/ha economic return over 50-year period (2016 estimate)
- **Watershed investment programs (LAC)**: Annual per-hectare costs vary >50-fold; programs with 10+ years of data average 0.25-3.02 million PPP-adjusted 2018 international dollars annually (median: 0.75 million)

### South Brazilian Grasslands (Pampa/Atlantic Forest transition)
- **Estimated cost range**: US$ 1,170-5,197 per hectare
- Variation depends on degradation scenario (light to severe)
- Lower cost than forest restoration but with different ecosystem service profiles
- Relevant to Porto Alegre's southern peri-urban areas

### Wetland Restoration (Banhados)
- **Estimated cost**: US$ 3,000-8,000 per hectare for constructed wetlands
- Natural wetland restoration generally less expensive than forest restoration
- High return on investment due to water filtration and flood attenuation services
- Limited Brazilian-specific cost data; global averages used as proxy

### Mangrove Restoration (Brazilian coast)
- **Global median**: US$ 8,143 per hectare (implementation cost)
- **Range**: US$ 9 to US$ 700,000+ per hectare (extreme variability)
- **Alternative estimate**: Median US$ 2,099/ha when excluding outliers
- **Benefit-cost ratio**: 6.83 to 10.50 depending on discount rate
- Not directly applicable to Porto Alegre (no mangroves) but relevant for Brazilian NBS portfolio benchmarking

### Amazon Rainforest
- **Approximate cost**: US$ 4,800 per hectare
- Relevant as a comparison benchmark for tropical forest restoration in Brazil

### Urban NBS (Green Infrastructure)
- **Rain gardens**: US$ 15,000-50,000 per unit (varies by size and design)
- **Bioswales**: US$ 50-200 per linear meter
- **Green roofs**: US$ 100-300 per m2 (intensive); US$ 50-150 per m2 (extensive)
- **Permeable pavement**: US$ 20-80 per m2 (premium over conventional)
- **Urban tree planting**: US$ 200-500 per tree (including establishment care)
- Note: Urban NBS costs significantly higher per unit area than landscape-scale restoration

## Cost Per Beneficiary

### Urban Flood Risk Reduction
- NBS flood mitigation estimated **5% cheaper** than equivalent grey infrastructure (concrete channels) based on Curitiba park-based approach
- Urban parks providing flood attenuation: benefits per household estimated at US$ 500-2,000/year in avoided flood damages
- Combined green-grey approaches often most cost-effective

### Water Quality Improvement
- Constructed wetlands for wastewater treatment: US$ 5-15 per person served per year (O&M)
- Riparian buffer restoration: estimated US$ 0.10-0.50 per cubic meter of water filtered
- Significantly cheaper than conventional treatment plant upgrades for diffuse pollution

### Urban Cooling
- Urban tree canopy expansion: 1-3 degrees C cooling in surrounding area
- Estimated health co-benefit: US$ 50-200 per person per year in reduced heat-related illness
- Air pollutant sequestration: Porto Alegre's urban forest sequesters 15.67 tonnes CO/year

## Emission Reductions

### Carbon Sequestration by NBS Type

| NBS Type | Sequestration Rate (tCO2e/ha/year) | Notes |
|----------|-------------------------------------|-------|
| Atlantic Forest restoration | 5-15 | Increases over first 20 years as forest matures |
| Wetland restoration | 2-8 | Variable; also avoids methane from degraded wetlands |
| Urban tree planting | 0.5-2 per tree over lifetime | Cumulative; varies by species and growth rate |
| Grassland restoration | 1-3 | Soil carbon accumulation; slower than forest |
| Green roofs | 0.5-2 per 100 m2 | Modest direct sequestration; greater energy savings |

### Cost-Effectiveness of Emission Reductions
- Forest restoration: US$ 5-50 per tCO2e (among the cheapest mitigation options)
- Urban NBS: US$ 50-200 per tCO2e (higher cost but multiple co-benefits)
- NBS generally most cost-effective when co-benefits (flood reduction, biodiversity, health) are monetized

### Comparison with Conventional Mitigation
- Solar energy: US$ 30-80 per tCO2e
- Energy efficiency: US$ 10-50 per tCO2e
- NBS competitive when full co-benefit accounting is applied

## Economic Return Benchmarks

### General Return on Investment
- For every GBP 1 spent on afforestation, estimated **GBP 2.79** returned in quantified economic and social benefits
- NBS benefit-cost ratios typically range from 2:1 to 10:1 depending on context and valuation methodology

### Job Creation
- Restoration activities generate approximately **0.42 jobs per hectare** on average
- Higher labor intensity than grey infrastructure, benefiting local employment
- Relevant for post-flood economic recovery in Porto Alegre

### Property Value Effects
- Urban parks and green spaces typically increase nearby property values by 5-20%
- Well-maintained parks (e.g., Parcao in Porto Alegre) show strongest effects
- Property value increases generate municipal tax revenue (IPTU), creating fiscal co-benefits

## Typical Financing Structures for Brazilian NBS Projects

### Public Funding Sources
- **BNDES**: Green credit lines for NBS, forest restoration, and environmental infrastructure
- **Fundo Clima**: Federal climate fund supporting mitigation and adaptation projects
- **FNMA**: National environmental fund for restoration and conservation
- **Municipal environmental funds**: FMMA in Porto Alegre
- **Multilateral loans**: World Bank, IDB, AIIB, CAF providing concessional financing

### Blended Finance Models
- Public funding covers planning, permitting, and initial implementation (typically 40-60% of total cost)
- Private sector participation through PPPs for O&M (e.g., Orla do Guaiba model)
- Carbon credit revenue for forest and wetland restoration (voluntary and compliance markets)
- Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES) schemes linking downstream beneficiaries to upstream restoration

### Brazil-Specific Initiatives
- **Floresta Viva (MMA)**: BRL 500+ million mobilized for restoration across all biomes
- **Brazil Restoration & Bioeconomy Finance Coalition**: Target US$ 10 billion by 2030 for forest conservation and bioeconomy
- **Tropical Forests Forever Facility (TFFF)**: Innovative conservation financing model proposed at COP30
- **National restoration target**: 12 million hectares, estimated cost US$ 5-9 billion over 10 years

### Carbon Market Revenue
- Voluntary carbon market prices for Brazilian forest restoration: US$ 10-30 per tCO2e
- Potential annual revenue per hectare of restored Atlantic Forest: US$ 50-450/year
- Carbon credits can improve project financial viability but rarely cover full restoration costs alone

### Key Financing Challenges
- **Standardization gap**: Lack of globally standardized NBS monitoring/measurement frameworks complicates investor confidence
- **Data requirements**: Well-defined baselines, robust monitoring, and transparent verification needed for investment readiness
- **Long payback periods**: NBS benefits accrue over decades, creating mismatch with typical investment horizons
- **Revenue uncertainty**: Ecosystem service valuations vary widely, making financial projections difficult
- **Transaction costs**: Small NBS projects face disproportionate preparation and due diligence costs
