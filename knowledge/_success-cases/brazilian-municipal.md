# Brazilian Municipal NBS Success Stories

## Curitiba - Park-Based Flood Management

### Project: Barigui River Basin Parks System
- **City**: Curitiba, Parana
- **Population**: ~1.9 million
- **Scale**: Network of urban parks along the Barigui River and tributaries covering thousands of hectares
- **Started**: 1996 as part of Metropolitan Area sanitation program

### Approach
- Central intervention: creation of large public parks around main river and floodplains
- Integrated Urban Drainage Master Plan expanded the strategy to tributary parks
- Parks serve dual function: public recreation and flood water retention
- Constructed wetlands within parks improve water quality
- Flood warning system integrated with park management
- Regulatory measures: new river construction standards, tax incentives for conservation area maintenance, integration with urban master plan

### Cost and Outcomes
- **Cost savings**: Estimated **5% cheaper** than equivalent concrete canal approach
- **Flood reduction**: Significant decrease in flood damage in protected areas
- **Green space**: Curitiba achieved 52 m2 of green space per capita (among highest in Brazil)
- **Co-benefits**: Recreation, property value increases, biodiversity habitat, urban cooling, tourism
- **Financial model**: Public investment in park creation; tax incentives for private conservation; integration with municipal planning budget

### NBS Lessons
- Nature-based flood management can be cheaper than grey infrastructure
- Park systems create political support through visible public amenities
- Long-term maintenance requires institutional commitment and dedicated funding
- Integration with master plan ensures regulatory protection of flood retention areas

## Curitiba - Urban Agriculture Network

### Project: Community Gardens Program
- **Scale**: Over 200 urban gardens across the city
- **Output**: Approximately 1,600 tonnes of fresh fruit and vegetables annually
- **Locations**: Under power lines, idle and underutilized urban sites

### Approach
- Municipal government enables citizen groups to establish gardens
- Gardens connect people with nature while providing food security
- Green infrastructure doubles as productive land
- Integration with waste management (composting programs)

### Outcomes
- Food security improvement for vulnerable populations
- Conversion of underutilized urban spaces to productive green areas
- Soil improvement and local biodiversity enhancement
- Community building and social cohesion

## Sao Paulo - Tiete River System Restoration

### Project: Tiete River Cleanup and Parque Ecologico do Tiete
- **City**: Sao Paulo, Sao Paulo
- **Population**: ~12.3 million (city), ~22 million (metro)
- **Scale**: 1,400 hectares ecological park + river system restoration
- **Started**: 1992 (first phase of cleanup); park established 1976

### Investment
- Over **US$ 3.4 billion** invested in river cleanup since 1992
- Recent PPP (2024-2025): **R$ 9.5 billion** 15-year public-private partnership for dredging, cleaning, and revitalizing the Tiete and Pinheiros rivers
- Homero Silva Square: spring restoration connecting to Tiete using Cerrado and Mata Atlantica species

### Outcomes
- Incremental improvement in water quality in upstream sections
- Ecological park provides flood attenuation, recreation, and environmental education
- Spring protection at source tributaries demonstrates watershed-scale NBS approach
- Urban forests promoted native ecosystem restoration within the metropolitan area

### Financial Model
- Public-private partnership for long-term O&M
- State government (SABESP) as primary investor
- IDB and World Bank financing for earlier phases
- Water tariff revenues partially fund infrastructure

### NBS Lessons
- Scale of investment reflects decades of environmental degradation - prevention is far cheaper than restoration
- PPP model can mobilize private capital for long-term NBS maintenance
- Watershed-scale approach needed for urban river restoration
- Political persistence required across multiple administrations

## Sao Paulo - Urban Agriculture and Food Security

### Project: Municipal Urban Agriculture Program
- **Scale**: 2,600+ mapped agricultural sites across the city
- **Role model**: Recognized by UNEP as an advanced approach to urban agriculture

### Approach
- Municipal programs provide bio-inputs, soil remineralizers, machinery access
- Training and investment support for urban farmers
- Integration with climate resilience (green infrastructure, soil improvement)
- Links to food security policy in vulnerable communities

### Outcomes
- Significant expansion of urban green productive areas
- Climate resilience through distributed green infrastructure
- Food security for vulnerable populations
- Soil health improvement in urban areas

## Recife - Capibaribe Park and Filtering Gardens

### Project: Parque Capibaribe (Linear Park)
- **City**: Recife, Pernambuco
- **Population**: ~1.6 million
- **Scale**: 60 hectares of riverbank areas with controlled flooding sectors
- **River**: Capibaribe

### Approach
- Linear park along the Capibaribe River as an ecological corridor
- Hydraulic open system managing higher water levels at lower cost than traditional flood defenses
- Filtering garden in a public park demonstrates natural water quality improvement
- Designed around the natural environment rather than resisting it (the city is built across three islands)
- NBS as driver for urban development and revitalization

### Outcomes
- Recovery of 60 hectares of riverbank areas
- Controlled flooding sectors reduce flood damage while maintaining ecological function
- Filtering garden pilot project demonstrated water quality improvement through natural processes
- Success sparked interest in new riverfront facilities (piers, public spaces)
- Catalyzed investments in sustainable mobility (river crossings, bike infrastructure)

### Financial Model
- Municipal investment with support from international cooperation
- Catalytic effect: initial NBS investment attracted follow-on private investment in surrounding areas
- Demonstration model: pilot success justified scaling up

### NBS Lessons
- "Design with nature" approach works even in highly constrained urban environments
- Pilot projects (filtering gardens) can build evidence and political support for scaling
- Linear parks along rivers combine ecological restoration with urban connectivity
- NBS can drive broader urban regeneration beyond the immediate project site

## Sao Paulo - Corrego Limpo Program

### Project: Stream Depollution Program
- **City**: Sao Paulo, Sao Paulo
- **Started**: 2007 (partnership between State Government, Prefeitura, and SABESP)
- **Scale**: 149 streams depolluted across ~200 km2, benefiting 2.2 million people

### Approach
- Systematic identification and elimination of illegal sewage connections discharging into urban streams
- Expansion of formal sewer network connections in parallel with stream cleanup
- Community engagement and environmental education
- Monitoring of water quality indicators to track progress

### Cost and Outcomes
- **Total investment**: R$ 220 million (SABESP) over 9 years (2007-2016)
- **Sewage flow removed from streams**: 1,500 liters per second
- **Streams treated**: 149 across 9 program phases
- **Beneficiary population**: 2.2 million people
- **Financial model**: State utility (SABESP) funding through water/sewer tariff revenues
- Program resumed in 2023-2024 after a hiatus

### NBS Lessons
- Stream restoration requires solving the root cause (sewage discharge) before ecological restoration can succeed
- Large-scale programs need sustained institutional commitment across political cycles
- Quantified outcomes (149 streams, 2.2M beneficiaries) build political credibility for continuation
- Program demonstrates the foundation needed before riparian NBS can be effective

Sources: Prefeitura de Sao Paulo, Water Action Hub, SABESP

## Belo Horizonte - Drenurbs Stream Rehabilitation

### Project: DRENURBS - Environmental Recovery and Sanitation of Valley Floors
- **City**: Belo Horizonte, Minas Gerais
- **Population**: ~2.5 million
- **Started**: 2003 (first phase: Corrego do Onca sub-basin, 2003-2010)
- **Funder**: IDB loan (BR-L1647) + municipal counterpart

### Approach
- Reversed the traditional approach of canalizing urban streams (channeling them underground)
- Prioritized green and blue infrastructure (GBI) as an alternative to concrete channels
- Stream daylighting, riparian restoration, linear park creation along restored waterways
- Integration of flood management with public space creation in underserved neighborhoods
- Environmental education centers within restored areas

### Cost and Outcomes
- **Water quality**: Significant improvements in in-stream habitat, riparian zones, and macroinvertebrate assemblages comparing pre- and post-interventions
- **Flood resilience**: Areas with GBI interventions proved much more resilient during January 2020 floods, while canalized areas suffered severe flooding
- **Social benefits**: Recreation space, biodiversity education, and green areas in previously underserved neighborhoods
- **Limitation**: Restoration results remained below natural reference conditions, indicating "restoration" to pristine state is unlikely in urban contexts

### Financial Model
- IDB concessional loan + municipal budget
- Linear parks generate property value uplift in surrounding areas
- Maintenance integrated into municipal parks budget

### NBS Lessons
- Green-blue infrastructure outperforms grey canalization for flood resilience (proven during 2020 floods)
- Nature-based drainage solutions deliver biodiversity and social co-benefits that concrete channels cannot
- A decade of experimentation was needed to establish solid policy framework for GBI prioritization
- Urban stream restoration is achievable but won't reach pristine reference conditions

Sources: IDB Project BR-L1647, Frontiers in Environmental Science (2022), The Nature of Cities (2020)

## Salvador - Coastal Mangrove and Urban Greening

### Project: Mangrove Conservation and Urban Green Corridors
- **City**: Salvador, Bahia
- **Population**: ~2.9 million
- **Context**: Coastal city facing combined pressures of urban expansion, mangrove degradation, and climate vulnerability

### Approach
- Protection and restoration of mangrove ecosystems along the Bay of All Saints (Baia de Todos os Santos)
- Integration of green corridors connecting urban parks with coastal ecosystems
- UNESCO MangRes Project (2022-2025) implementing mangrove restoration as nature-based solution in biosphere reserves

### Challenges and Outcomes
- Bahia mangroves under strong human pressure, with largest areas coinciding with densely populated zones
- Protected areas established but several lack implementation and management
- Community-based restoration efforts engaging local fishing communities
- Tourism value of restored coastal ecosystems (kayaking, birdwatching, education)

### NBS Lessons
- Coastal NBS requires balancing conservation with urban development pressure
- Protected area designation alone is insufficient without active management and enforcement
- Community engagement with fishing communities provides both ecological knowledge and social buy-in

Sources: UNESCO MangRes Project, ScienceDirect (Mangrove Park of Recife), OneEarth

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### Common Success Factors
1. **Integration with urban planning**: Most successful cases embed NBS in master plans and zoning
2. **Visible public amenities**: Parks and green spaces create political support that pure infrastructure lacks
3. **Institutional permanence**: Long-term success requires dedicated agencies or strong institutional mandates
4. **Multi-functionality**: Projects delivering flood control + recreation + biodiversity + food security attract broader support
5. **Pilot-then-scale**: Demonstration projects (Recife filtering garden, Orla Phase 1) build evidence for expansion
6. **Community ownership**: Participatory processes in Curitiba and Porto Alegre strengthen maintenance and stewardship

### Common Challenges
1. **Political discontinuity**: Municipal election cycles (4 years) create risk of policy reversal
2. **Maintenance funding**: Initial investment easier to secure than ongoing O&M budgets
3. **Land acquisition**: Urban land costs make large-scale NBS expensive in central areas
4. **Monitoring gaps**: Limited long-term data on NBS performance weakens the evidence base
5. **Social equity**: NBS benefits often accrue to wealthier neighborhoods with better parks; equitable distribution requires deliberate policy

### Applicability to Porto Alegre
- **Curitiba's park-based flood management** is directly applicable to Porto Alegre's post-flood reconstruction, especially for Arroio Diluvio and low-lying areas
- **Recife's linear park model** relevant for Regenera Diluvio project
- **Sao Paulo's PPP financing** applicable to scaling the Orla do Guaiba model
- **Urban agriculture networks** from Curitiba and Sao Paulo applicable to food-insecure neighborhoods affected by floods
- Porto Alegre's existing participatory budgeting tradition provides stronger community governance than most comparison cities
