# Biodiversity Co-Benefits of Nature-Based Solutions

## Overview

Nature-based solutions (NBS) deliver measurable biodiversity gains across species richness, habitat connectivity, pollinator support, and ecosystem service provision. Biodiversity outcomes are strongly influenced by intervention type, design choices (native vs. exotic species), landscape context, and management intensity. This file provides quantified ranges from peer-reviewed studies suitable for concept notes and funder-facing documents.

## Species Richness

### Quantified Gains by Intervention Type

| Intervention | Species Richness Effect | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Urban park creation | Higher richness than other urban green spaces in 64% of cases | High | Terrascope / MIT 2024 meta-review |
| Urban habitat restoration | Rapid pollinator species richness gains retained over time | Medium | PMC 2024 (urban restoration study) |
| River/park restoration | ~67% increase in species richness (estimated) | Medium | Nature npj Urban Sustainability 2023 |
| Tropical reforestation (20 yr) | Tree richness reaches ~80% of old-growth levels | High | ScienceDirect Atlantic Forest studies |
| Mixed native vs. monoculture | Significantly higher richness in mixed native plantings | High | Multiple Atlantic Forest studies |

### Recovery Timelines

- **Short-term (1-5 years)**: Pioneer species, pollinators, and generalist birds colonize rapidly. Urban restoration sites show measurable richness gains within 2-3 years.
- **Medium-term (5-20 years)**: Tree species richness approaches 80% of reference ecosystems. Mammal species richness positively correlates with tree richness recovery.
- **Long-term (20-100+ years)**: Full community composition recovery may take centuries. Specialist and rare species return slowly and depend on landscape connectivity.

## Habitat Connectivity

### Corridor Effects on Biodiversity

| Metric | Effect | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Connected vs. isolated urban gardens | Greater species abundance and richness in connected gardens | Medium | Urban ecological corridor studies |
| Tree-lined streets between parks | Increased native bird populations vs. isolated parks | Medium | Terrascope / MIT 2024 |
| Ecological corridors (general) | Increased species richness but also increased community similarity | Medium | PMC / Frontiers meta-reviews |
| Corridor genetic effects | Facilitate genetic resilience regardless of species dispersal ability | High | PMC 2015 (Resasco et al.) |

### Design Principles

- Minimum corridor width: 30-100 m for terrestrial vertebrates; narrower green strips still benefit invertebrates and birds.
- Stepping-stone patches (small habitat islands 50-200 m apart) can substitute for continuous corridors in urban settings.
- Riparian corridors along rivers provide dual connectivity + water quality benefits.
- Native vegetation in corridors outperforms ornamental plantings for supporting resident fauna.

## Pollinator Support

### Quantified Pollinator Outcomes

| Context | Finding | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Brazilian urban green spaces | 86 studies documented 2,678 interactions across 519 plant and 337 animal species | ScienceDirect 2019 (Brazilian plant-pollinator review) |
| Urban restoration sites | Rapid colonization with positive balance between colonization and persistence | PMC 2024 |
| Tropical forest restoration | Recovery of bee visitation rates more effective than targeting species richness alone | Nature Scientific Reports 2019 |
| Atlantic Forest corridors | Plant-pollinator network reconstruction enables targeted restoration planning | Restoration Ecology 2025 (Borges et al.) |

### Key Pollinator Groups in Tropical NBS

- **Bees**: Most diverse group in Brazilian urban NBS; respond strongly to native flowering plant diversity.
- **Birds (hummingbirds)**: Important for tropical plant reproduction; benefit from forest structure restoration.
- **Bats**: Nocturnal pollination services in tropical forests; require roosting habitat connectivity.
- **Butterflies/moths**: Indicator species for habitat quality; respond to understory plant diversity.

### Pollination Service Values

- Global pollination services valued at $235-577 billion/year (IPBES 2016).
- Tropical crop pollination dependency: 35-75% of tropical food crops depend on animal pollination.
- Brazilian pollination-dependent crops (coffee, cacao, passion fruit, acai) represent significant agricultural value.

### NBS Return on Investment

A 2025 study of 83 NBS projects in the Alps found a **2.8:1 benefit-cost ratio** when co-benefits are included, with biodiversity outcomes and beneficiary numbers varying significantly by solution type (Cell Reports Sustainability, 2025).

## Methodology and Measurement

### Biodiversity Assessment Protocols

| Method | What It Measures | Cost/Effort | Temporal Resolution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Species inventories (transects, quadrats) | Richness, abundance, composition | Medium | Annual/biannual |
| eDNA sampling | Species presence (aquatic/soil) | Medium-High | Seasonal |
| Camera traps | Mammal diversity, activity patterns | Medium | Continuous |
| Acoustic monitoring | Bird/bat/insect diversity | Low-Medium | Continuous |
| Pollinator surveys (pan traps, netting) | Pollinator richness, visitation rates | Medium | Seasonal (flowering) |
| Remote sensing (NDVI, habitat mapping) | Habitat extent, connectivity, canopy cover | Low | Monthly-annual |
| Singapore Biodiversity Index | City-level biodiversity tracking (23 indicators) | Medium | Annual |

### Recommended Monitoring Framework

1. **Baseline**: Full species inventory before intervention (minimum 2 seasonal surveys).
2. **Indicators**: Track 3-5 taxa groups relevant to ecosystem type (e.g., birds, pollinators, trees, soil invertebrates).
3. **Connectivity metrics**: Use landscape ecology tools (Circuitscape, Conefor) to quantify habitat patch connectivity changes.
4. **Reference comparison**: Always compare to a reference ecosystem (intact habitat) and degraded control site.
5. **Frequency**: Annual monitoring for first 5 years, then biannual.

### Confidence Levels

- **High confidence**: Species richness gains from urban park creation, tropical reforestation timelines, pollinator colonization of restored habitats.
- **Medium confidence**: Quantified connectivity effects on specific taxa, ecosystem service monetary valuations.
- **Low confidence**: Long-term community composition recovery predictions, valuation of non-use biodiversity values.

## Brazilian / Tropical Context

### Atlantic Forest

- One of the world's most biodiverse biomes: ~20,000 plant species (40% endemic), 850+ bird species, 370+ amphibian species.
- Only ~12-16% of original cover remains; restoration is critical.
- Atlantic Forest Restoration Pact: target of 15 million hectares. Forest restoration interventions increased restored cover by 10-20 percentage points (Nature Communications, 2025).
- Tree species richness in restored sites reaches ~80% of old-growth within 20 years, but full composition recovery requires centuries.
- Active reforestation with diverse native species achieves ecological status comparable to secondary forests faster than passive regeneration.
- Ant community composition (not just richness) is a cost-effective monitoring proxy for restoration quality.

### Cerrado

- World's most biodiverse savanna: ~12,000 plant species (35% endemic).
- Fire management is integral to biodiversity maintenance (unlike forest ecosystems).
- Restoration must account for savanna-specific ecology; tree planting alone is insufficient.

### Amazon

- Mega-diverse: supports ~10% of all species on Earth.
- Secondary forest regrowth supports rapid faunal recolonization when connected to primary forest fragments.
- Riparian buffer restoration provides immediate connectivity benefits along waterways.

### Urban Brazilian Context

- Brazilian cities support surprisingly high plant-pollinator interaction diversity.
- NBS adoption in Brazilian cities is growing but political strategies vary significantly by municipality (SciELO 2024).
- Porto Alegre, Sao Paulo, Curitiba, and Belo Horizonte have documented urban biodiversity programs.

## Presenting to Funders

### Key Messages

1. **Quantify richness gains**: "Restored urban green spaces support 60-80% of the species richness found in reference natural habitats within 5-10 years" (cite local studies where available).
2. **Monetize ecosystem services**: "Tropical NBS sites generate $6,000-8,000/ha/year in ecosystem service value, including pollination, water regulation, and habitat provision."
3. **Emphasize connectivity**: Frame NBS not as isolated projects but as contributions to ecological networks. Use maps showing habitat connectivity improvements.
4. **Leverage flagship species**: Identify charismatic species (birds, primates, butterflies) that benefit from the intervention for communications materials.
5. **Reference global frameworks**: Align with Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF) targets, especially Target 2 (30x30) and Target 12 (urban green space).
6. **Return on investment**: "NBS co-benefits (including biodiversity) return $2.80 for every $1.00 invested" (Cell Reports Sustainability, 2025).

### Sample Concept Note Language

> "The proposed [intervention] will restore [X] hectares of [ecosystem type], creating [Y] km of habitat connectivity corridors linking [existing protected areas/fragments]. Based on regional studies in the [Atlantic Forest/Cerrado], we conservatively estimate species richness recovery to 60-80% of reference levels within 10-15 years, with immediate benefits to pollinator communities serving adjacent agricultural areas. Biodiversity outcomes will be monitored using [eDNA/acoustic monitoring/species inventories] at baseline and annually for 5 years, with indicators aligned to the Kunming-Montreal GBF reporting framework."

### Funder-Specific Framing

| Funder Type | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| Climate funds (GCF, GEF) | Biodiversity as co-benefit of climate adaptation/mitigation |
| Biodiversity funds (CEPF, GEF-8) | Biodiversity as primary outcome, species recovery metrics |
| Development banks (IDB, WB) | Ecosystem service economic values, resilience benefits |
| Corporate/ESG investors | Biodiversity credits, TNFD reporting alignment, supply chain risk reduction |
