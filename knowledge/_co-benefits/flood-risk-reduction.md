# Flood Risk Reduction by Nature-Based Solutions

## Overview

Nature-based solutions provide measurable flood risk reduction through stormwater capture, peak flow attenuation, infiltration enhancement, and floodplain reconnection. Over 80% of reviewed studies find NBS more cost-effective than conventional engineering for disaster risk reduction (NBS Initiative / Oxford).

### Combined / Integrated NBS Systems

| Metric | Value | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rainwater harvesting + porous pavement + bioretention: annual runoff | 41% reduction | Medium | ScienceDirect |

**Key finding**: NBS effectiveness decreases for higher return-period storms. NBS are most effective for frequent, small-to-medium rainfall events. For extreme events (50-100 year), NBS should complement (not replace) conventional grey infrastructure.

### Property-Level Damage Reduction

- Floodplain restoration can reduce flood damages to nearby properties by 20-40%
- Upstream wetland retention reduces downstream flood peaks, protecting assets
- Green infrastructure retrofit programs in combined-sewer cities reduce overflow events by 50-80%

## Methodology and Measurement

### Hydrological Modeling

- **Curve Number method**: Standard approach for estimating runoff reduction from land cover changes
- **SWMM (EPA Storm Water Management Model)**: Industry standard for urban stormwater NBS modeling
- **HEC-HMS / HEC-RAS**: US Army Corps models for watershed-scale flood analysis
- **MIKE models**: European standard for integrated urban drainage modeling

### Performance Monitoring

- Flow gauges (inlet/outlet) for volume and peak flow measurement
- Soil moisture sensors for infiltration monitoring
- Water level loggers for storage capacity tracking
- Rainfall-runoff analysis with pre/post-intervention comparison
- Minimum 2-3 years of post-construction monitoring recommended

### Key Design Parameters

- **Water quality volume (WQV)**: Typically first 25mm of rainfall
- **Channel protection volume (CPV)**: Extended detention of 12-48 hour storms
- **Flood control volume**: 10-100 year design storms (NBS contributes but rarely handles alone)

## Brazilian / Tropical Context

### Porto Alegre Floods (2024)

- Record floods in May 2024 affected ~80% of population, demonstrating critical need for NBS
- Lack of upstream retention and floodplain connectivity amplified downstream impacts
- Post-disaster reconstruction presents opportunity to integrate NBS into recovery

### Brazilian Urban Context

- High-intensity tropical rainfall (>60mm/hr common) challenges NBS sizing
- Fortaleza wetland system: 9 interconnected ponds using bioremediation, benefiting ~70,000 people
- Atlantic Forest domain: NBS for flood reduction evaluated in second-most urbanized region
- Brazilian cities increasingly adopting NBS roadmaps (EU-Brazil Sector Dialogue)

### Design Considerations for Tropical Cities

- Size NBS for tropical rainfall intensities (typically 1.5-2x temperate design depths)
- Account for seasonal rainfall patterns (wet/dry seasons)
- Select species tolerant of both flooding and drought
- Consider informal settlements and equity in NBS placement
- Integrate with existing drainage systems (hybrid grey-green approach)

## Presenting to Funders

### Key Messages

1. **Lead with avoided damages**: Express benefits in dollars of avoided flood damage per dollar invested
2. **Multiple return periods**: Show performance across 2-yr, 10-yr, 25-yr, and 100-yr storms
3. **Acknowledge limitations**: NBS complements grey infrastructure for extreme events; it does not replace it
4. **Emphasize co-benefits**: Every flood NBS intervention also delivers water quality, carbon, biodiversity, and amenity benefits
5. **Reference Porto Alegre**: For Brazilian proposals, the 2024 floods are a powerful motivating example

### Sample Concept Note Language

> "The proposed green infrastructure network, comprising [X] bioretention cells, [Y] m2 of permeable surfaces, and [Z] hectares of restored wetlands, is designed to reduce peak stormwater flows by [A-B]% for storms up to the 10-year return period, capturing approximately [C] m3 of stormwater per event. Based on World Bank cost-effectiveness guidance and comparable projects, this represents a benefit-cost ratio of [D]:1, with avoided flood damages estimated at USD [E] million over a 20-year project lifetime."
