# Carbon Sequestration by Nature-Based Solutions

## Overview

Nature-based solutions (NBS) offer significant carbon sequestration potential across multiple intervention types. Rates vary substantially by ecosystem type, climate zone, management practice, and stand age. This file provides quantified ranges suitable for concept note preparation and funder-facing documents.

## Sequestration Rates by Intervention Type

### Tropical Reforestation and Afforestation

| Intervention | Rate (tCO2e/ha/yr) | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Planted tropical forests (first 20 yrs) | 4.5 - 40.7 | High | Global CO2 removal rates meta-analysis (Cook-Patton et al.) |
| Natural regeneration (tropical) | 3.0 - 11.0 | High | WRI / Griscom et al. 2017 PNAS |
| Agroforestry systems (tropical) | 2.0 - 9.0 | Medium | IPCC AR4 WG3 Ch.9 |
| Urban green infrastructure (mixed) | ~2.16 (avg across 35 Chinese cities) | Medium | ScienceDirect 2024 |

**Key finding**: IPCC Tier 1 defaults underestimate carbon sequestration in young tropical forests by ~50% (Cook-Patton et al., Carbon Balance and Management).

### Mangrove Restoration

| Metric | Value | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Carbon burial rate | 6.4 tCO2/ha/yr (174 gC/m2/yr) | High | Donato et al., Tandf 2012 |
| 10-year cumulative sequestration | 28.7 - 70.0 tCO2/ha | Medium | Indonesia restoration study (Nature Scientific Reports 2025) |
| Standing carbon stock | ~3,437 tCO2e/ha (937 tC/ha avg) | High | Multiple reviews |
| Sediment accretion rate | ~5 mm/yr | High | Donato et al. |

Mangroves are among the most carbon-dense ecosystems on Earth. Restoration avoids emissions from degradation and adds new sequestration capacity.

### Wetland and Peatland Restoration

| Intervention | Rate (tCO2e/ha/yr) | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Peatland carbon accumulation (intact) | 0.07 - 0.18 (20-50 kgC/ha/yr) | Medium | IPCC Wetlands Supplement 2013 |
| Avoided emissions from peatland drainage (tropical) | 20 - 55 (avoided loss) | High | IPCC Wetlands Supplement 2013 |
| Constructed wetlands | 1.0 - 5.0 | Low-Medium | Variable by design and climate |

**Important note**: For tropical peatlands, the primary carbon benefit is avoiding emissions from drainage and degradation rather than new sequestration. Tropical peatlands store ~89 Pg C globally. Rewetting drained tropical peatland can avoid 20-55 tCO2e/ha/yr in emissions.

## Methodology and Measurement

### IPCC Tier Approaches

- **Tier 1**: Uses global default emission factors. Simplest approach but least accurate. Underestimates tropical forest sequestration by ~32-50%.
- **Tier 2**: Country-specific emission factors incorporating forest age, species composition, and climate zone. Recommended for project-level estimates.
- **Tier 3**: Process-based models with site-specific data. Most accurate but resource-intensive.

### Recommended Approach for Concept Notes

1. Use **Tier 2** values where available (national forest inventories, country-specific IPCC factors)
2. Apply **conservative estimates** (lower quartile of published ranges) for credibility with funders
3. Note **additionality**: sequestration beyond the baseline/counterfactual scenario
4. Specify **permanence period**: 20-100 year accounting windows (IPCC standard: 20 years for forest growth)
5. Include **leakage risk**: discount by 10-20% for potential displacement effects

### Monitoring Protocols

- Remote sensing (Landsat, Sentinel-2) for canopy cover change
- Ground-truth plots (permanent sample plots with 5-year remeasurement cycles)
- Allometric equations (species-specific biomass equations)
- Soil carbon sampling (0-30cm depth, baseline + periodic remeasurement)
- IPCC Good Practice Guidance for LULUCF

## Brazilian / Tropical Context

### Atlantic Forest Restoration

- Natural regeneration in Atlantic Forest: 5-12 tCO2e/ha/yr in first 20 years
- Brazil's Atlantic Forest Restoration Pact: target of 15 million hectares
- Mixed native species plantations outperform monocultures for carbon and biodiversity

### Amazon and Cerrado

- Secondary forest regrowth in Amazon: 6-15 tCO2e/ha/yr
- Cerrado savanna restoration: 1.5-4.0 tCO2e/ha/yr (lower due to ecosystem type)
- REDD+ and avoided deforestation: avoided emissions of 150-500+ tCO2e/ha (stock protection)

### Brazilian Mangroves

- Brazil has the second-largest mangrove area globally (~1.3 million ha)
- Restoration potential concentrated in northeast coast and southern estuaries
- National mangrove carbon factors available from SIRENE/MCTI

### National Emission Factors

- Brazil's National GHG Inventory (SIRENE) provides country-specific factors
- Fourth National Communication provides updated LULUCF emission factors
- PRODES/INPE satellite monitoring provides deforestation baselines

## Presenting to Funders

### Key Messages

1. **Use conservative ranges**: Present lower-bound estimates as "minimum expected" and upper-bound as "potential under optimal conditions"
2. **Monetize where possible**: At $5-50/tCO2e (voluntary market range), reforestation of 100 ha could generate $2,250 - $200,000/yr in carbon value
3. **Emphasize co-benefits**: Carbon sequestration is rarely the sole benefit; always pair with flood reduction, biodiversity, etc.
4. **Reference credible standards**: Gold Standard, Verra VCS, Plan Vivo for methodology alignment
5. **Acknowledge uncertainty**: Funders value transparency about confidence levels

### Sample Concept Note Language

> "Based on IPCC Tier 2 emission factors and regional studies, the proposed [intervention] across [X] hectares is conservatively estimated to sequester [Y-Z] tCO2e per year over a 20-year period, representing a total mitigation potential of [total] tCO2e. These estimates are based on [methodology] and will be verified through [monitoring protocol]."
