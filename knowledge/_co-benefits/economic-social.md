# Economic and Social Co-Benefits of Nature-Based Solutions

## Overview

Nature-based solutions generate significant economic returns and social benefits beyond their primary environmental functions. Co-benefits typically represent the largest share of total NBS value, with a recent comprehensive study finding a 2.8:1 benefit-cost ratio across 83 NBS projects (Cell Reports Sustainability, 2025). This file quantifies property value effects, green job creation, tourism revenue, water treatment savings, and social cohesion outcomes.

## Property Values

### Quantified Property Value Premiums

| Green Infrastructure Type | Price Premium | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Proximity to parks (general) | Up to 20% increase | High | Hedonic pricing meta-analysis |
| Urban parks (within 100 m) | +5.4% per hectare of park area | High | Tandfonline 2024 |
| Urban parks (aggregate effect) | +19.2% | High | Meta-analysis (37 hedonic studies) |
| Urban forests | +17.1% | High | Meta-analysis |
| Water bodies (lakes, rivers) | +10-20% | Medium | Meta-analysis of hedonic studies |
| Street trees (mature canopy) | +3-7% per property | Medium | Multiple US and European studies |
| Green roofs (visible) | +2-5% for adjacent properties | Low-Medium | Limited studies |

### Distance Decay Effect

- Premium is highest within 100 m of green space and decays logarithmically.
- Measurable effect extends to 300-500 m from parks; negligible beyond ~1 km.
- For each 10 m decrease in distance to open space, property values increase by a measurable percentage.
- Urban parks are more highly valued than other urban open space types (forests, agricultural land, undeveloped land).

### Variation by Context

- **High-density neighborhoods**: Larger premiums due to scarcity of green space.
- **Population density**: Positive correlation between property premium and local population density.
- **Park quality**: Well-maintained parks with amenities generate higher premiums than neglected green space.
- **Tropical cities**: Limited hedonic pricing studies in Brazilian/Latin American contexts; transferability from US/European studies requires caution (discount 20-30% for conservative estimates).

### Brazilian Context

- Brazil's real estate market increasingly recognizes green amenity premiums, particularly in Sao Paulo, Curitiba, and Porto Alegre.
- "Parque" proximity is a recognized selling point in Brazilian property markets.
- Informal settlements (favelas) near restored green spaces may face gentrification/displacement risk; equity safeguards needed.

## Green Jobs

### Jobs Per Million Dollars Invested

| NBS Activity | Jobs per $1M USD | Skill Level | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Forest restoration / sustainable management | ~40 direct + indirect | Medium | US-based studies (WRI) |
| Coastal habitat protection | ~17 direct | Medium | Land Trust Alliance / EESI |
| Forestry stimulus (developing countries) | ~275 direct | Low-Medium | ILO / UNEP 2020 |
| Nature-based solutions (global average) | 7-40 | Mixed | ILO Nature HIRES report |
| Conventional infrastructure (comparison) | 5-15 | Medium-High | Multiple infrastructure studies |

### Global Employment Projections

- Currently ~75 million people work in NBS-related activities globally, with 96% in lower-middle income countries in Asia-Pacific (ILO/IUCN/UNEP 2024).
- NBS employment could increase by up to 32 million new jobs globally by 2030 with targeted investment (ILO/UNEP Decent Work in NbS 2024 report).
- 98-99% of current NBS jobs are in agriculture and forestry sectors.
- Greatest gains projected in Africa, Latin America, and Arab States: from 2.5 million to 13+ million NBS jobs.
- Most NBS jobs are currently medium-skilled; higher-skilled roles (monitoring, design, management) projected to grow.
- Important caveat: there is currently no guarantee NBS employment meets ILO "green jobs" standards (decent work, fair wages, safety); investments in skills training are essential.

### Job Quality Considerations

- NBS jobs do not automatically meet ILO "green jobs" standards (decent work, fair wages, safety).
- Skills gaps exist in both technical (ecology, hydrology, GIS) and core competencies.
- Training and certification programs needed to ensure job quality alongside job quantity.
- Community-based restoration programs can combine employment with traditional knowledge preservation.

### Brazilian Context

- Brazil's National Restoration Plan (PLANAVEG) targets 12 million hectares; potential for 1-3 million restoration jobs.
- Atlantic Forest Restoration Pact engages hundreds of organizations and thousands of workers.
- Rural-urban linkage: restoration jobs in peri-urban areas can reduce rural-urban migration pressure.
- Existing seedling nursery networks in southern Brazil provide foundation for NBS supply chain jobs.

- Brazil's ecotourism sector generated ~$6 billion/year pre-pandemic; NBS can expand this.
- Restored Atlantic Forest fragments attract birdwatching tourism (Brazil has 1,900+ bird species).
- Urban parks in Porto Alegre, Curitiba, and Florianopolis are significant tourism attractions.
- Mangrove restoration creates kayaking, birdwatching, and educational tourism opportunities.

## Water Treatment Savings

### Constructed Wetlands vs. Conventional Treatment

| Metric | Constructed Wetlands | Conventional Treatment | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Operating cost per m3 | $0.03-0.08 | $0.30-1.50 | Brown & Caldwell; multiple studies |
| Capital cost per hectare | ~$18,200 (free water surface) | Varies widely by capacity | US EPA / Brown & Caldwell |
| Cost ratio (CW vs. conventional) | ~97% lower total treatment costs | Baseline | Comparative cost studies |

### Watershed Protection Savings

| Example | Savings | Context | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| New York City watershed protection | $7 billion avoided infrastructure costs | Protected Catskill/Delaware watersheds | Land Trust Alliance / EESI |
| Protected vs. unprotected watershed treatment | 10x cost difference | General finding | Multiple water utility studies |
| Raw water from wetland vs. reservoir | $0.17-0.30/m3 vs. $0.41-1.12/m3 | Constructed wetland sources | Brown & Caldwell |

### Brazilian Context

- Porto Alegre's water supply from Guaiba River is vulnerable to pollution and sedimentation; watershed NBS can reduce treatment costs.
- Brazilian water utilities (DMAE in Porto Alegre, SABESP in Sao Paulo) increasingly recognize NBS for water security.
- Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES) programs (e.g., Programa Produtor de Agua by ANA) compensate upstream landowners for watershed protection.
- Constructed wetlands are well-suited to Brazilian climate (year-round biological activity in tropical/subtropical zones).

## Social Cohesion

### Quantified Social Outcomes

| Outcome | Evidence | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Community connectedness | Greater sense of belonging and trust in tree-dense neighborhoods | Medium | USFS / 3+ studies |
| Social interaction frequency | Increased positive social interactions in and around green spaces | Medium | PMC 2019 review |
| Community gardens | Foster food security, social networks, and cultural identity | Medium | Multiple urban agriculture studies |
| Crime reduction | 7-12% reduction in property crime near urban green spaces | Medium | US city studies (Philadelphia, Baltimore) |
| Environmental justice | Targeted NBS in underserved areas reduces exposure inequality | Medium-High | Multiple environmental justice studies |

### Social Cohesion Mechanisms

1. **Shared spaces**: Parks and green areas create neutral meeting points across socioeconomic lines.
2. **Participatory design**: Community involvement in NBS planning builds social capital and agency.
3. **Cultural services**: Green spaces support cultural and spiritual practices, recreation, and education.
4. **Food security**: Urban agriculture and agroforestry NBS address food access in food deserts.
5. **Identity and place attachment**: Restored landscapes strengthen community identity and sense of place.

### Brazilian Context

- Community-based restoration programs in Brazilian cities combine ecological outcomes with social integration.
- Urban agriculture in Sao Paulo (Programa de Agricultura Urbana) and Belo Horizonte provide models for NBS social cohesion.
- Indigenous and traditional community involvement in restoration brings cultural knowledge and strengthens rights recognition.
- Potential gentrification risk: property value increases from NBS can displace low-income residents; mitigation requires affordable housing policies alongside greening.

## Methodology and Measurement

### Economic Valuation Methods

| Method | Application | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hedonic pricing | Property value premiums | Market-based, well-established | Requires housing data, doesn't capture non-market values |
| Travel cost method | Recreation/tourism value | Reveals use values | Misses non-use values, data-intensive |
| Contingent valuation (CVM) | Willingness-to-pay for NBS | Captures non-market values | Hypothetical bias, survey design sensitivity |
| Benefit transfer | Rapid estimates from existing studies | Fast, low-cost | Accuracy depends on site comparability |
| Cost-effectiveness analysis | NBS vs. grey infrastructure | Decision-relevant, intuitive | Doesn't monetize all benefits |
| Social Return on Investment (SROI) | Comprehensive social/economic value | Holistic, stakeholder-inclusive | Complex, requires extensive data |

### Social Impact Measurement

| Tool | What It Measures | Cost/Effort |
|---|---|---|
| Community surveys (pre/post) | Social cohesion, wellbeing, trust, safety | Medium |
| Participatory mapping | Community priorities, spatial equity | Low-Medium |
| Economic multiplier analysis | Local employment and spending effects | Medium |
| Crime statistics (police records) | Crime rate changes near NBS sites | Low |
| Park usage counts | Recreation participation, demographic equity | Low-Medium |

### Recommended Monitoring Framework

1. **Property values**: Track assessed values within 500 m of NBS sites vs. control areas (annual, using municipal tax records).
2. **Employment**: Count direct jobs created (construction, maintenance) and indirect jobs (supply chain, tourism) quarterly.
3. **Water treatment**: Monitor influent/effluent quality at NBS treatment sites; compare operating costs to conventional alternatives.
4. **Social surveys**: Baseline and annual community surveys measuring social cohesion indicators (trust, participation, sense of belonging).
5. **Tourism/recreation**: Visitor counts and spending surveys at NBS sites (seasonal).

### Confidence Levels

- **High confidence**: Property value premiums from hedonic pricing meta-analyses, NYC watershed savings, NBS benefit-cost ratio (2.8:1).
- **Medium confidence**: Green jobs per $1M invested, social cohesion improvements, tourism revenue estimates.
- **Low confidence**: Long-term gentrification effects, quantified social cohesion monetary values, developing country benefit transfer accuracy.

## Presenting to Funders

### Key Messages

1. **Return on investment**: "NBS co-benefits return $2.80 for every $1.00 invested, with economic and social benefits representing the largest share of total value" (Cell Reports Sustainability, 2025).
2. **Property values**: "Properties within 100 m of urban parks see 5-20% value increases, generating expanded municipal tax revenue that can partially fund NBS maintenance."
3. **Job creation**: "NBS investments create 7-40 jobs per $1 million, 2-5x more than conventional grey infrastructure, with particular potential in developing countries (up to 275 jobs/$1M)."
4. **Water savings**: "Watershed protection through NBS costs 10x less than treating water from unprotected sources; constructed wetlands operate at 97% lower cost than conventional treatment."
5. **Social inclusion**: "Community-designed NBS builds social capital while addressing environmental justice gaps in underserved neighborhoods."
6. **Multiple SDGs**: NBS economic-social benefits align with SDG 1 (Poverty), SDG 6 (Water), SDG 8 (Decent Work), SDG 10 (Inequality), SDG 11 (Cities).

### Sample Concept Note Language

> "The proposed NBS intervention is projected to generate economic returns of $[X-Y] million over 20 years through property value enhancement, water treatment savings, and recreation value, against an investment of $[Z] million (estimated BCR: 2.5-3.0:1). The project will create approximately [N] direct and indirect jobs during the 3-year implementation phase and [M] permanent maintenance positions. Community engagement through participatory design will strengthen social cohesion in [target neighborhood], with outcomes tracked through annual social impact surveys and economic monitoring aligned to SROI methodology."

### Funder-Specific Framing

| Funder Type | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| Development banks (IDB, WB, CAF) | BCR, job creation, poverty reduction, municipal revenue |
| Climate funds (GCF, GEF) | Cost-effectiveness vs. grey infrastructure, adaptation economics |
| Impact investors | SROI, measurable social outcomes, blended value |
