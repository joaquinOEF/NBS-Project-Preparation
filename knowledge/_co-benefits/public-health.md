# Public Health Co-Benefits of Nature-Based Solutions

## Overview

Nature-based solutions deliver substantial public health benefits through air quality improvement, mental health enhancement, physical activity promotion, and heat-related mortality reduction. These co-benefits are increasingly quantified in peer-reviewed literature, enabling evidence-based arguments for NBS investment. Health co-benefits are particularly significant in tropical/developing city contexts where baseline exposure to environmental health risks is high.

## Air Quality (PM2.5 Reduction)

### Quantified Removal Rates

| Intervention | PM Reduction | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Urban vegetation (general) | 16.5-26.7% reduction in PM concentrations | Medium | Bayesian meta-analysis, Springer 2022 |
| Street-level greening (15-60 m radius) | Highly variable; can restrict ventilation in canyons | Low | PNAS 2024 (reassessment study) |
| Urban forest canopy | 7-24% local PM2.5 reduction | Medium | PMC meta-analysis 2022 |
| Green walls / vertical greening | Highest benefit-cost ratio for PM removal in dense urban areas | Medium | Taipei City NBS evaluation |
| Tree belts (roadside, >30 m width) | 20-50% PM reduction downwind | Medium | Multiple wind-tunnel and field studies |

### Important Caveats

- **Street-canyon effect**: Dense tree canopy in narrow streets can trap pollutants by reducing ventilation. Net effect depends on street width-to-height ratio and canopy density.
- **Species selection matters**: Broadleaf evergreen species (high leaf area index) capture more PM than deciduous or coniferous species. Some species emit biogenic volatile organic compounds (BVOCs) that contribute to ozone formation.
- **Scale dependency**: City-wide canopy benefits are robust; street-level effects are context-specific. Modelling is the dominant assessment method (51.9% of studies).
- **Seasonal variation**: In tropical climates, evergreen species provide year-round benefits, a significant advantage over temperate deciduous canopies.

### Health Impact Quantification

| Metric | Value | Context | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Premature deaths prevented (US) | ~850/year from urban forests | National scale, 2010 data | USFS i-Tree / Nowak et al. |
| Acute respiratory incidents avoided | ~670,000/year | US urban forests, 2010 | USFS i-Tree |
| Monetary health value of US urban forests | ~$7 billion/year | Air quality benefits only | USFS i-Tree |
| Premature deaths avoided per tree | 1 per 100 trees planted (30-year period) | Portland, Oregon longitudinal study | Yale Climate Connections 2023 |
| Health cost savings (Taipei City) | NT$943 million ($30M USD) | PM2.5 reduction by urban vegetation | ScienceDirect NBS evaluation |

### Dose-Response Relationships

- Each 10 ug/m3 increase in PM2.5 is associated with a 6-13% increase in all-cause mortality (WHO, Global Burden of Disease).
- Urban tree planting that reduces local PM2.5 by 1-3 ug/m3 can generate measurable population-level health benefits in cities with high baseline PM2.5 (>25 ug/m3).
- Benefits are largest in neighborhoods with highest baseline pollution and lowest existing canopy cover (environmental justice co-benefit).

## Mental Health

### Quantified Mental Health Benefits

| Outcome | Effect Size | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anxiety reduction (green space exposure) | Significant decrease in anxiety, depression, anger, confusion, fatigue | High | PMC scoping review, 201 studies |
| Stress recovery (wooded environments) | Greater effects in individuals with higher initial stress levels | High | Multiple experimental studies |
| Depression risk reduction | Lower incidence in neighborhoods with higher tree density | Medium | USDA / US Forest Service review |
| Cognitive function restoration | Improved attention and mental cognition after green space exposure | Medium | PMC 2020 scoping review |
| Community mental wellbeing | Greater sense of connectedness, belonging, trust in tree-dense areas | Medium | 3+ studies cited in USFS review |

### Mechanisms

1. **Attention Restoration Theory (ART)**: Natural environments restore directed attention fatigue. Urban green spaces provide "soft fascination" that allows cognitive recovery.
2. **Stress Reduction Theory (SRT)**: Physiological stress markers (cortisol, blood pressure, heart rate) decrease within 15-20 minutes of green space exposure.
3. **Social facilitation**: Green spaces increase social interaction, reducing isolation and loneliness (linked to depression and cardiovascular risk).
4. **Noise reduction**: Urban vegetation buffers reduce perceived and actual noise levels by 3-10 dB, reducing noise-related stress.

### Tropical/Brazilian Context

- Heat stress amplifies mental health burden in tropical cities; shade from NBS provides compounded mental + thermal benefits.
- Community garden programs in Brazilian favelas show social cohesion and mental wellbeing improvements (qualitative evidence growing).
- Access inequality: low-income neighborhoods in Brazilian cities typically have 40-60% less green space per capita; targeted NBS addresses environmental justice.

## Physical Activity

### Quantified Physical Activity Outcomes

| Factor | Effect | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Proximity to parks/green space | Increased physical activity, decreased obesity | Medium-High | PMC / USDA reviews |
| Cardiovascular disease incidence | Lower in neighborhoods with higher tree density | Medium | Multiple epidemiological studies |
| Walking/cycling increase | 10-30% increase in active transport near green corridors | Medium | Multiple urban planning studies |
| BMI reduction | Small but significant population-level effect from park access | Medium | US and European cohort studies |

### NBS Design for Physical Activity

- **Linear parks and greenways**: Most effective for promoting walking/cycling (continuous routes of >1 km).
- **Park accessibility**: Benefits strongest when green space is within 300-500 m of residences.
- **Shade and thermal comfort**: Critical in tropical climates; unshaded parks see reduced use during hot periods.
- **Safety and lighting**: Perceived safety is the primary barrier to park use in many Brazilian urban contexts.

## Heat-Related Mortality Reduction

### Quantified Heat-Mortality Reduction

| Intervention | Mortality Reduction | Confidence | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Urban tree canopy (30% coverage) | 2-4C surface temperature reduction | High | Multiple i-Tree and satellite studies |
| Portland 30-year tree planting | 1 premature death avoided per 100 trees | Medium | Longitudinal observational study |
| Urban parks (>10 ha) | 1-3C cooling extending 100-300 m beyond park edge | Medium | Park cool island studies |
| Green roofs | 0.5-2.0C building interior temperature reduction | Medium | European and Asian studies |
| Street trees (mature canopy) | 5-15C surface temperature reduction under canopy | High | Thermal imagery studies |

### Heat-Health Pathway

1. **Exposure reduction**: NBS canopy shading reduces outdoor surface temperatures by 5-15C and ambient air by 1-4C.
2. **Indoor cooling**: Green roofs and wall vegetation reduce building energy demand for cooling by 10-30%.
3. **Vulnerable population protection**: Heat mortality disproportionately affects elderly (65+), outdoor workers, and children. NBS in these areas has highest health impact.
4. **Night-time cooling**: Parks and green spaces cool faster than built-up areas, reducing the "urban heat island at night" effect that drives heat mortality.

### Tropical City Relevance

- Brazilian cities face increasing heat waves (frequency doubled in many cities 2000-2025).
- Heat-related mortality in Sao Paulo estimated at 5,000+ excess deaths/year during extreme events.
- Porto Alegre recorded record temperatures during 2023-2024 events; NBS cooling is directly relevant to project preparation context.
- Equitable cooling: NBS in low-income neighborhoods provides cooling to those least likely to have air conditioning (5-15% AC penetration in Brazilian favelas vs. 60-80% in formal neighborhoods).

## Methodology and Measurement

### Health Impact Assessment Methods

| Method | Application | Data Requirements | Complexity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Health Impact Assessment (HIA) | Regulatory/policy evaluation | Population data, exposure models, dose-response functions | High |
| i-Tree Eco model | Urban forest PM removal and health valuation | Tree inventory, local meteorology, pollution monitoring | Medium |
| BenMAP (EPA) | Air quality health benefit estimation | PM2.5 concentration changes, population data, baseline health rates | High |
| WHO HEAT tool | Physical activity economic valuation | Walking/cycling counts, mortality data | Low-Medium |
| Hedonic/stated preference | Willingness-to-pay for health benefits | Survey data, property records | Medium |

### Recommended Monitoring Indicators

1. **Air quality**: PM2.5 concentration at NBS sites vs. control (low-cost sensor networks).
2. **Temperature**: Surface and ambient temperature differentials (thermal sensors, satellite LST).
3. **Park usage**: Pedestrian counters, intercept surveys for physical activity.
4. **Health records**: Hospital admissions for heat stroke, respiratory illness in intervention vs. control areas (requires health system partnership).
5. **Mental health**: Validated survey instruments (WHO-5, PHQ-9) in community samples before/after NBS implementation.

### Confidence Levels

- **High confidence**: PM removal by urban vegetation at city scale, heat-mortality association, stress reduction from green space exposure.
- **Medium confidence**: Quantified deaths avoided per tree, specific mental health effect sizes, physical activity increases from park proximity.
- **Low confidence**: Long-term cardiovascular outcomes attributable to specific NBS interventions, street-level PM2.5 effects in tropical urban canyons.

## Brazilian / Tropical Context

### Climate-Health Nexus

- Tropical cities face compounded health risks: high baseline temperatures, air pollution from traffic and biomass burning, vector-borne disease (dengue, Zika).
- NBS can address multiple risks simultaneously: shade reduces heat exposure, water management reduces mosquito breeding (if designed correctly; stagnant water in poorly designed NBS can increase vector risk).
- Rainy season flooding drives waterborne disease; NBS flood mitigation has direct health co-benefits.

### Brazilian Health Data Sources

- DATASUS (national health information system): Hospitalization and mortality data by municipality.
- IBGE Census: Population demographics, socioeconomic data for health equity analysis.
- INMET: Meteorological data for heat-health modeling.
- CETESB (Sao Paulo), FEPAM (Rio Grande do Sul): State-level air quality monitoring networks.

### Porto Alegre Specific

- 2024 catastrophic floods highlighted acute public health vulnerabilities.
- Urban heat island effect documented at 3-8C differential between center and periphery.
- Air quality monitoring shows PM2.5 exceedances during winter inversions and biomass burning periods.
- Low-income neighborhoods (Restinga, Lomba do Pinheiro) have lowest canopy cover and highest heat exposure.

## Presenting to Funders

### Key Messages

1. **Lives saved**: "Every 100 urban trees planted prevents approximately 1 premature death over their lifetime through air quality and heat reduction benefits" (cite Portland longitudinal study).
2. **Economic value**: "Urban forest health benefits are valued at $7 billion/year in the US alone; tropical cities with higher pollution baselines can expect proportionally greater per-capita returns."
3. **Equity argument**: "NBS targeted to underserved neighborhoods delivers the largest health gains per dollar invested, as these communities face the highest baseline exposure to heat, pollution, and flood risk."
4. **Multiple pathways**: Frame NBS health benefits across all four pathways (air quality, mental health, physical activity, heat) to demonstrate compounding returns.
5. **SDG alignment**: Link to SDG 3 (Good Health), SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities), SDG 13 (Climate Action).
6. **Climate adaptation**: "As climate change increases heat wave frequency and intensity, NBS provides passive, low-maintenance cooling infrastructure that reduces strain on health systems."

### Sample Concept Note Language

> "The proposed urban greening intervention in [neighborhood] will plant [X] trees and create [Y] hectares of green space, providing measurable public health co-benefits. Based on peer-reviewed evidence, we estimate: (1) local PM2.5 reductions of 15-25% within 200 m of interventions; (2) surface temperature reductions of 3-8C under canopy; (3) increased park-based physical activity benefiting [Z] residents within 500 m. Health outcomes will be monitored through [air quality sensors/DATASUS hospitalization records/community health surveys] over a 5-year period, with economic valuation using WHO HEAT and BenMAP methodologies."

### Funder-Specific Framing

| Funder Type | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| Climate adaptation funds (GCF, AF) | Heat-mortality reduction, climate resilience for vulnerable populations |
| Health-focused (WHO, Wellcome Trust) | Air quality, mental health, physical activity evidence base |
| Development banks (IDB, WB, CAF) | Economic valuation of avoided health costs, equity dimensions |
| Bilateral (GIZ, USAID, DFID) | SDG co-benefits, community health resilience |
