# Inclusive Climate Action — Participatory Frameworks for NBS

## Why Funders Care

The BPJP/C40 concept note template has 6 dedicated fields on inclusive action (section 11). This is not optional — it's a scoring criterion. Projects that demonstrate genuine community engagement and equity considerations score significantly higher.

## Key Questions the Concept Note Must Answer

### 1. Vulnerable Area Inclusion
- Does the project include areas carentes (disadvantaged) or periféricas (peripheral)?
- How were these areas identified?
- What specific vulnerabilities exist (flood-prone, heat-exposed, low-income)?

### 2. Community Participation (Affected Community)
- Was there dialogue with residents during project design?
- How was it structured? (assembleias, oficinas, consultas públicas, surveys)
- If not yet done, what's the plan?

### 3. Vulnerable Group Participation
- Which groups were/will be consulted?
- How will their perspectives shape the solution?
- Specific engagement methods per group

### 4. Specific Group Mapping
Groups the template explicitly asks about:
- Mulheres (women)
- Idosos (elderly)
- Pessoas com deficiência (people with disabilities)
- Comunidades tradicionais (traditional communities)
- Jovens (youth)
- Pessoas negras (Black population)
- Populações indígenas (Indigenous peoples)

### 5. Equity Diagnostic
- Was a formal diagnostic conducted to identify inequalities?
- What methodology was used?
- What specific needs were identified?

### 6. Engagement & Communication Strategy
- Beyond the formal processes above, any other strategies?
- Communication channels
- Feedback mechanisms
- Ongoing participation structure

## Best Practice Patterns for NBS Projects

### Community-Centered Design
- **Map vulnerable populations** within the intervention zone using census data + municipal social assistance records
- **Identify differentiated impacts** — e.g., women often bear more burden during floods (caregiving, evacuation logistics)
- **Co-design green spaces** with communities to ensure they serve actual needs (shade, recreation, food production, safety)

### Participatory Methods That Work
| Method | When to Use | Scale |
|---|---|---|
| Community assemblies (assembleias) | Early scoping, priority setting | Neighborhood (50-200 people) |
| Design workshops (oficinas) | Solution design, site selection | Small groups (15-30) |
| Walking interviews | Site-specific feedback, accessibility audit | Individual/small group |
| Household surveys | Baseline data, needs assessment | Large scale (statistical) |
| Digital platforms | Ongoing feedback, voting on options | City-wide |
| Community monitoring | Post-implementation MRV | Trained local residents |

### Green Jobs & Training
- NBS projects create **10-30 direct jobs per hectare** during establishment (planting, site prep)
- **3-8 permanent jobs per hectare** for maintenance (more for complex systems like constructed wetlands)
- Prioritize hiring from affected communities
- Include training programs: nursery management, tree care, wetland maintenance, environmental monitoring

### Brazilian Legal Framework for Participation
- **Estatuto da Cidade** (Law 10.257/2001): mandates participatory processes for urban planning
- **PNMA** (Law 6.938/1981): requires environmental impact assessments with public hearings
- **Municipal master plans**: must include participatory elaboration (audiências públicas)
- **Conselho Municipal de Meio Ambiente**: formal participation channel

## How to Present to Funders

1. **Be specific** — "We conducted 3 community workshops with 150 residents in [bairro]" beats "We plan to engage the community"
2. **Name the groups** — explicitly list which vulnerable groups were/will be included
3. **Show the feedback loop** — how community input actually changed the project design
4. **Quantify benefits distribution** — "60% of direct beneficiaries are from low-income households (income < 2 SM)"
5. **Include a participation timeline** — show engagement happens at every stage, not just the beginning
